A War Of Words



Humanity has a problem. Pollution. We're addicted to it. One of the largest contributors to this addiction is our love of plastics and the convenience it presents to us in our daily lives. Plastics are used in construction and medical equipment. One might say this would be the 'good' which plastics can bring. Plastics are also used to neatly package items we consume each and every day. We even use plastics to carry around these neatly packaged items. Plastics provide much of humanity a sense of entitlement, as it becomes more ingrained in our lives. Plastics have made us lazy.

What happens when we become lazy? What happens to the plastic after we finish that sandwich? That bottle of water? That straw we use? It ends up in our creeks, streams, rivers, and oceans.

Plastic enters the oceans from coastlines, rivers, tides, and marine sources. But once it is there, where does it go? The distribution and accumulation of ocean plastics is strongly influenced by oceanic surface currents and wind patterns. Plastics are typically buoyant – meaning they float on the ocean surface - allowing them to be transported by the prevalent wind and surface current routes. As a result, plastics tend to accumulate in oceanic gyres, with high concentrations of plastics at the centre of ocean basins, and much less around the perimeters. After entry to oceans from coastal regions, plastics tend to migrate towards the centre of ocean basins.

The light at the end of the tunnel

I've made the situation so far, sound as if it were purely doom and gloom. Don't get me wrong, it's a very real problem.
But what I've failed to mention thus far, is the credit that is due to the organizations, teams, and individuals out there who are actually doing something to help solve our problems.

In our journey to retrieve data, we came across two of many organizations who are doing some fantastic work.

  • The Ocean Cleanup - a non-profit organization, is developing advanced technologies to rid the world’s oceans of plastic.
  • TIDE - a public data system containing the world's largest ocean trash dataset, all collected by volunteers.
